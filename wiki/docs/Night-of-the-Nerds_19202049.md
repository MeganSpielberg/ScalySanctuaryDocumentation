# Night of the Nerds

> ℹ️ **Note:** Author: Megan

## 🛠️ Preparation

Night of the Nerds is a public showcase event at Fontys where students
exhibit their projects to visitors, fellow students, and staff. It
presented an opportunity to run a large number of participant sessions
in a single day, making it the most significant data collection moment
of the semester alongside the smaller in-person sessions that preceded
it.

Preparation began weeks in advance. Eight laptops and mice were reserved
through the ISSD, and the day before the event, Valentino and I went
through every machine individually - logging into eduroam, running
Windows updates, installing Chrome, and verifying the game ran correctly
on each station. Both of our personal laptops were kept on standby as
backup. Andy, a friend already familiar with the project, was recruited to help
facilitate on the day; he received a full briefing beforehand and
brought his own laptop as an additional fallback, bringing the total
backup capacity to three machines.

## 🖥️ Setup

The eight testing stations were arranged across two tables of four, with
a separate recruitment laptop at a dedicated onboarding point where
participants were greeted, screened, and assigned to a condition before
being directed to a station. The version tracking log was maintained
throughout the day to monitor condition counts, dropouts, and which
condition was currently active on each station.

## 🎉 The Day

The event started slowly. Visitors were largely walking past without
stopping, and those who were approached often declined immediately. It
became clear within the first hour that most of the passing visitors
were too young to meet the study's eligibility criteria. The strategy
shifted: rather than waiting for visitors to approach or trying to
intercept the general flow, we began approaching fellow ICT students who
were exhibiting their own projects nearby. During quieter periods
between visitor waves, these students were invited to participate, and
this became the primary source of participants throughout the day.

For younger visitors who wanted to take part but did not meet the age
requirement, laptops 7 and 8 were configured with a debug build of the
game. No data was collected from these sessions, and the surveys were
skipped, allowing them to experience the game without contaminating the
dataset. Eligible participants who agreed to take part after being told
the session would take approximately 20 minutes were given a brief
verbal introduction and a printed procedure overview before starting.

## 📊 Result

By the end of the day, over 30 participants had completed sessions at
the event, contributing to a final total of 100 participants across all
collection moments - exceeding the target of 80 set by the power
analysis. The event was the single largest contributor to that total.

The environment, however, was not well-suited to a research study. The
space was noisy, crowded, and frequently interrupted, with other
exhibits competing for attention in the same room. Participants were
completing knowledge tests and playing the game under conditions that
were meaningfully different from the quieter in-person sessions earlier
in the semester. This is worth acknowledging as a potential confound in
the data, particularly for engagement scores, which may have been
influenced by external distraction rather than the condition itself.

## 🪞 Reflection

Night of the Nerds demonstrated both the value and the limitations of
opportunistic data collection. The volume of participants it enabled
was essential - without it, hitting 100 would not have been possible.
But the tradeoff was a noisier environment than a controlled study
ideally calls for. In hindsight, leaning more heavily on the in-person
sessions and using the event primarily as a supplementary rather than
central collection moment might have produced cleaner data. That said,
the power analysis target was met, and the condition distribution
across all sessions remained balanced enough to support the planned
analysis.
