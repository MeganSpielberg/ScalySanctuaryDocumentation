# Devlog: Reptile Handling

> ℹ️ **Note:** Author: Megan

## 💡 Intro

One of the early design questions for Scaly Sanctuary was how to teach
players the physical side of reptile caretaking. Handling is one of the
most visible ways beginners interact with their animals, and it is also
one of the most visibly mishandled. A quick look at reptile communities
online makes this clear: animals grabbed by the tail, restrained
incorrectly, or handled far too early after arrival. This is especially
common with beginner-friendly species like leopard geckos, which are
cheap and widely available, meaning they often end up with owners who
have done very little research. That pattern motivated the decision to
build a dedicated petting module as one of Scaly Sanctuary's core
gameplay scenes.

## 🔬 Research

The rationale for the module was straightforward: improper handling
causes measurable stress in reptiles, and leopard geckos in particular
have well-documented comfort and stress zones. The tail, for example,
should never be grabbed as geckos can drop it as a defense response.
The back and belly respond differently to scooping versus poking. This
kind of zone-specific knowledge is exactly the type of practical,
actionable information the game was designed to teach, making it a
natural fit for an interactive scene rather than a static information
screen.

## ⚙️ Implementation

The petting scene translates this handling knowledge directly into
gameplay. The player selects a hand mode (Grab, Scoop, or Poke) from a
toolbar, then interacts with different parts of the gecko's body. The
system responds based on a reaction matrix that cross-references body
zone with hand mode, producing either a happy or stressed outcome.
Getting it wrong triggers an audible gecko scream, making the
consequence of mishandling immediate and memorable rather than
abstract.

Under the hood, four scripts divide the responsibilities cleanly.
`petting_scene.gd` acts as the orchestrator: it listens for interaction
events from the lizard, tallies happy and stressed outcomes, pushes
those counts to the `GameFlowManager` after each interaction, and
handles save and load state via `GameStateHelper` signals. It also
detects which scene variant is active (Base, Vet, or LLM) and routes to
the correct tutorial script accordingly, meaning the same script drives
three different experiences without duplication.

`handmanager.gd` is a minimal state machine that manages the cursor
tool. It holds four modes (GRAB, SCOOP, POKE, NEUTRAL) and swaps the OS
cursor texture on every mode change, giving the player clear visual
feedback about which hand they are using. The three toolbar buttons
connect directly to `set_mode()`, keeping the interaction model simple
and responsive.

`interactive_lizard.gd` handles the hit-testing and reaction logic. It
tracks mouse hover across four `CapsuleShape2D` zones (head, tail,
belly, and back) using a manual point-in-capsule test, then on mouse
release, cross-references the active zone with the current hand mode to
determine the outcome. Stressed results additionally notify an optional
`vet_manager`, enabling the Vet and LLM scene variants to trigger a
contextual response from the NPC.

`species_petting_info.gd` functions as a static lookup table, storing
comfort zones, stress zones, and handling tips for the leopard gecko and
bearded dragon, with a generic fallback. This data populates the
in-scene clipboard, giving players a reference resource without
breaking the flow of the interaction.

The reaction matrix that ties it all together:

| Body zone | Hand mode | Outcome |
|----|----|----|
| Head | Grab | Happy |
| Back | Scoop | Happy |
| Belly | Scoop | Happy |
| Tail | Poke | Stressed |
| Tail | Grab | Stressed |
| Head | Poke | Stressed |
| Anything else | — | No reaction |

## 🪞 Reflection

Despite how much went into building the petting module, it was
ultimately cut from the research prototype following an expert
interview with reptile care specialist Mariah Healy. Her feedback was
direct: feeding and terrarium setup are the areas where beginner owners
most commonly cause harm, and those are the domains where an educational
game can have real impact. Petting, by contrast, primarily benefits the
owner rather than the animal's welfare. Building a module around it
risks implying it is a meaningful part of responsible caretaking when
the evidence points elsewhere.

This was the right call, and it illustrates something important about
how the project was designed: research and expert input had genuine
authority over development decisions, not just advisory influence. The
petting module was replaced by a terrarium setup module, which better
aligns with where beginner knowledge gaps actually exist and directly
supports the study's educational goals. The technical work was not
wasted - the scene architecture, the variant-aware tutorial system, and
the save integration all carried forward into the rest of the game's
development.
